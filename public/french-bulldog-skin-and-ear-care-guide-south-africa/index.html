French Bulldog skin and ear care: a practical South African guide

If your Frenchie keeps scratching, smells "yeasty", or shakes their head often, you are not imagining it. Skin and ear flare-ups are common in this breed, and quick action usually means less pain and lower vet bills.

Why this happens so often in Frenchies

Flat-faced breeds like French Bulldogs are known to have higher health risks, including skin fold infections and breathing difficulties. UFAW highlights these as significant welfare concerns for the breed.

Many Frenchies also deal with sensitive skin linked to allergies, which can trigger repeated skin and ear infections if not managed early and consistently.

Early warning signs to watch for this week

  • Persistent paw licking or chewing
  • Head shaking, ear scratching, or a sour/yeasty smell
  • Redness inside skin folds (face, tail pocket, groin, armpits)
  • Greasy skin, flaky patches, or recurring hot spots
  • Tearing eyes, sneezing, or reverse sneezing during flare-ups

If symptoms keep returning, book a vet visit rather than repeatedly trying new products. Recurrent ear and skin issues often need a proper diagnosis and a long-term plan.

Simple routine that helps most owners

1) Keep folds clean and dry

Wipe folds gently with a dog-safe product, then dry properly. Damp folds are where irritation and infection can build quickly.

2) Clean ears on a schedule

Use a vet-approved, pH-balanced ear cleaner. Never use cotton buds deep in the canal. If your dog cries or resists badly, stop and call your vet.

3) Track flare-up triggers

Note food changes, grass exposure, new shampoos, weather shifts, and stress. A simple notes log helps your vet find patterns faster.

4) Protect breathing while managing skin

On hot or humid days, keep care sessions short and calm. Frenchies overheat quickly, so avoid doing grooming in direct midday heat.

When to treat this as urgent

  • Ear swelling, bleeding, or severe pain
  • Strong odour plus dark discharge from ears
  • Raw, open, or bleeding skin in folds
  • Fever, lethargy, or refusing food
  • Any breathing strain during a flare-up

Phone your vet immediately if these appear. Home care is for mild issues — not painful or worsening infections.

If the care burden is becoming too much

Ongoing skin and ear treatment can be emotionally and financially draining. Asking for help early is responsible, not shameful. It gives your Frenchie a safer path before things become a crisis.
