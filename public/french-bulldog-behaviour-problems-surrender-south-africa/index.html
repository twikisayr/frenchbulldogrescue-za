French Bulldog behaviour problems and surrender in South Africa

If your Frenchie has bitten, is fighting with pets, guarding food, panicking when left alone, or becoming unsafe at home, ask for help with clear facts. Honest behaviour history protects everyone and gives your dog the best chance of a safer plan.

When behaviour makes rehoming urgent

Bites or snaps

Include who was involved, what happened immediately before, whether skin was broken, and whether this has happened before.

Dog or cat conflict

Say which animals are affected, whether the Frenchie starts or receives conflict, and whether separation is currently possible.

Children or visitors

Be specific about growling, lunging, chasing, guarding, fear, or unsafe moments around children, guests, workers, or family members.

Separation distress

Describe barking, destruction, house-soiling, escape attempts, self-injury, or panic when left alone.

What to do before contact

  • Separate your Frenchie from children, visitors, or pets who may be at risk.
  • Avoid punishment, shouting, forced handling, or repeated “testing” of triggers.
  • Keep meals, toys, beds, and high-value items managed if guarding is a concern.
  • Write a short timeline of incidents while details are fresh.
  • If anyone is injured or the dog is medically unwell, contact an appropriate professional urgently.

This is practical safety guidance, not a diagnosis or behaviour treatment plan.

Behaviour details that help rescue triage

The surrender checklist already asks for a behaviour summary because matching and foster planning depend on honest context. Add the clearest version you can:

  • Triggers you have noticed: food, toys, handling, visitors, dogs, cats, children, pain, travel, or being left alone
  • Frequency: once, occasionally, weekly, daily, or escalating
  • Severity: warning signs only, snapping, bites, fights, escape attempts, or injury
  • Management that currently works: gates, crates, separate feeding, quiet rooms, muzzle training, or supervised-only contact
  • Medical context: pain, breathing issues, skin discomfort, recent surgery, medication, or sudden behaviour change

Do not hide bite or aggression history

Honesty is safer than optimism.

A hidden behaviour history can lead to the wrong home, unsafe transport, failed foster placement, or avoidable injury. Clear information does not mean judgement; it helps the team plan responsibly.
