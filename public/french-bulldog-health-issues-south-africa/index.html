French Bulldog health issues in South Africa

French Bulldogs are brachycephalic (flat-faced) dogs with a range of breed-specific health conditions that every owner in South Africa should understand. Knowing what to watch for can make a significant difference to your Frenchie's quality of life — and help you make informed decisions if health problems become difficult to manage.

BOAS — Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome

BOAS is the most significant health issue affecting French Bulldogs. It includes narrowed nostrils, an elongated soft palate, and a small trachea — all of which make breathing difficult. Signs include noisy breathing, snoring, gagging, exercise intolerance, and collapse in hot weather. In South Africa's warm climate, BOAS is especially dangerous. Surgical correction (BOAS surgery) costs R15,000–R30,000 and can dramatically improve quality of life. If your Frenchie is struggling to breathe, see our emergency breathing and heat guide immediately.

Heat sensitivity and heatstroke

French Bulldogs cannot cool themselves effectively because of their flat faces and compromised airways. In South African summer temperatures above 28°C, a Frenchie can overheat in minutes. Signs include heavy panting, drooling, bright red gums, vomiting, and collapse. Emergency cooling and veterinary treatment are critical. Never leave a Frenchie in a warm car, even with windows cracked. Always provide cool, shaded areas and fresh water. See our emergency guide for step-by-step heatstroke first aid.

Skin fold dermatitis

Those adorable wrinkles come with a cost. The skin folds on a Frenchie's face, tail, and elsewhere trap moisture and bacteria, leading to recurring skin fold dermatitis. This causes redness, irritation, smell, and discomfort. Regular cleaning with medicated wipes helps, but many Frenchies need ongoing treatment. In severe cases, surgery to reduce folds may be recommended. See our skin and ear care guide for practical management tips.

Ear infections

French Bulldogs have narrow ear canals that do not drain well, making them prone to chronic ear infections. Yeast and bacterial infections are common and often recur. Signs include head shaking, scratching at the ears, redness, discharge, and a bad smell. Treatment costs R400–R1,200 per episode, and recurrent cases may require ear flushing under anaesthetic. Keep ears dry and clean, especially after bathing or swimming.

Cherry eye

Cherry eye is a prolapse of the third eyelid gland, appearing as a red swelling in the corner of the eye. It is common in French Bulldogs and usually requires surgical correction (R3,000–R7,000 per eye). Left untreated, it can lead to dry eye and other complications.

IVDD — Intervertebral Disc Disease

French Bulldogs are chondrodystrophic (short-legged) dogs, which puts them at high risk for IVDD — spinal disc herniation. Symptoms include back pain, wobbliness, dragging back legs, or sudden paralysis. Treatment ranges from strict crate rest (R2,000–R5,000 in vet visits) to emergency spinal surgery (R20,000–R40,000). Prevent jumping on and off furniture by using ramps, and keep your Frenchie at a healthy weight.

Allergies

French Bulldogs are prone to environmental and food allergies. Symptoms include itchy skin, recurrent ear infections, paw licking, and hair loss. Allergy testing costs R2,000–R8,000, and management often involves prescription diets, immunotherapy injections, or regular medication. It is a lifelong condition for many dogs.

Other common concerns

  • Hip dysplasia: Abnormal hip joint development causing pain and lameness. Managed with medication, weight control, or surgery.
  • Patellar luxation: Kneecap dislocation, common in small breeds. Mild cases need only monitoring; severe cases require surgery.
  • Eye issues: Corneal ulcers, dry eye, and entropion (eyelids turning inward) are all more common in Frenchies because of their prominent eyes.
  • Anal gland problems: Impacted anal glands are common and need regular expression by a vet or groomer.

If health issues are overwhelming you

Caring for a French Bulldog with chronic health problems is financially and emotionally draining. If you are struggling to keep up with vet bills or the daily care needs of your Frenchie, you are not alone.
